## Purpose

CAIRE — Cognitive Architecture for Intuitive Reasoning Exploration — is the
second phase of MASS, running parallel to [AREA](program-overview.md). Where
AREA studies what multi-agent systems **do** — how they coordinate, fail,
escalate, and reason under varying conditions — CAIRE studies what they
**are** at the level of cognitive architecture: how agents' information-
handling profiles differ, what those differences predict about behavior, and
whether the differences are stable enough to be systematically assigned and
measured.

## Relationship to AREA

The two programs are designed to inform each other. AREA's findings
constrain what CAIRE's theory can responsibly claim — if AREA observes that
agents never reliably change a behavior regardless of how they're
instructed, CAIRE cannot build a framework that assumes that behavior is
producible through instruction alone. Conversely, CAIRE's findings enrich how
AREA's behavioral observations are interpreted — if CAIRE establishes that
certain instruction profiles reliably produce specific processing
signatures, AREA's behavioral findings can be re-examined through that lens
to ask not just what happened, but why.

## Theoretical Foundation

CAIRE draws on two parallel frameworks treated as jointly productive rather
than competing:

- **From cognitive science**: a well-established theory of cognitive
  function — first developed by Carl Jung and later formalized as part of
  the Myers-Briggs framework — describing distinct information-processing
  orientations along two dimensions: perception versus judgment, and
  externally-anchored versus internally-anchored reasoning.
- **From computer science**: AREA's behavioral schema paradigm, which
  describes instruction priming as a way of biasing an AI model's attention
  toward a particular subset of its own knowledge, rather than adding or
  removing anything from it. The externally/internally-anchored distinction
  in the cognitive-science framework maps onto this same idea: some
  processing orientations draw their reference point from the immediate
  environment, others from an internal model.

## Core Hypothesis

> If instruction priming functions as this kind of dynamic, attention-level
> biasing, then cognitive processing profiles — which specify an agent's
> reference frame and processing orientation — should produce reliably
> detectable, theory-consistent differences in agent behavior.

CAIRE's studies test whether those differences are large enough, consistent
enough, and theory-consistent enough to be treated as a valid empirical
basis for cognitive function expression in AI systems. This is the
prerequisite AREA's own hypothesis does not establish on its own: AREA shows
that the *structural quality* of an instruction predicts coordination
quality; CAIRE asks whether the *processing orientation* an instruction
assigns can be reliably produced and detected at all.
